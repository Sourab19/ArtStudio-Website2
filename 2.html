The Weeping Woman

Year: 1937

Author

Pablo Picasso

The Weeping Woman is a series of oil-on-canvas paintings by Pablo Picasso, created in 1937 and inspired by the Spanish Civil War. The works, featuring Dora Maar, Picasso's muse, respond to the bombing of Guernica and are intertwined with the imagery of his iconic painting, Guernica. Picasso was captivated by the theme of a weeping woman and revisited it throughout the year, culminating in the most intricate version on October 26, 1937, now housed in the Tate collection. This piece is currently on loan to the Kaohsiung Museum of Fine Arts in Taiwan as part of the Capturing the Moment exhibition. Another version, painted on October 18, 1937, resides in the National Gallery of Victoria and was once involved in a high-profile art theft.

The series reflects Picasso’s protest against the atrocities of the Spanish Civil War, particularly the bombing of Guernica on April 26, 1937, carried out by the German Luftwaffe on Franco’s behalf. These paintings serve as a personal outcry against the suffering inflicted on Spain. Picasso’s engagement with this theme began with The Dream and Lie of Franco, a series of etchings accompanied by a prose poem written in January 1937, which prefigured the Weeping Woman motif. In this poem, Picasso captured the collective anguish of a nation, symbolized by the cries of women, children, and everyday objects—a vivid testament to the harrowing impact of war.
